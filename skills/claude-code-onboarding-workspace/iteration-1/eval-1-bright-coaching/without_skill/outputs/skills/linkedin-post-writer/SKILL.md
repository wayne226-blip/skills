# LinkedIn Post Writer

Write LinkedIn posts for Bright Coaching (Sarah Chen).

## Before Writing

- Read BRAND.md for tone, voice rules, and banned phrases
- Read BUSINESS.md for Sarah's expertise and audience

## Sarah's LinkedIn Voice

Sarah writes like she talks — direct, warm, no fluff. She shares real observations from her coaching work (anonymised), calls out bad leadership habits without being preachy, and gives practical advice that busy managers can use immediately.

**Always:**
- Short sentences. Fragments are fine.
- Contractions: "don't" not "do not"
- First person — Sarah is the author
- One idea per paragraph. White space matters.
- Real examples from coaching (anonymised — never use real client names)

**Never:**
- Hype words: game-changing, revolutionary, powerful, leverage
- Performative openers: "I've been reflecting on..."
- Corporate signposting: "In this post I will..."
- Generic closers: "What do you think?" as the only ending
- Hashtag spam: 3 max, specific ones only
- Starting with a question

## Post Structure

**LINE 1-2 (THE HOOK)** — Shows before "...see more". Must earn the click.

**BODY (3-7 short paragraphs)** — Deliver on the hook. Real coaching insights, practical advice. No padding.

**LANDING (2-3 lines)** — A real insight or specific question. The last line sticks.

**HASHTAGS** — 3 max. Specific: #leadershipcoaching, #techleadership, #managertips. Never generic: #success, #mindset.

## Two Versions Required

### Version A — Results / Insight Hook
Lead with a concrete observation, result, or pattern from coaching.

Examples:
- "Every manager I've coached this year has had the same problem. It's not what you'd expect."
- "One conversation changed how my client runs every meeting. Here's what she did differently."

### Version B — Counterintuitive or Honest Admission Hook
Open with something that challenges a common management assumption.

Examples:
- "The best managers I work with don't motivate their teams. Here's what they do instead."
- "I coach leaders for a living, and I still get this wrong sometimes."

## Before You Finalise

- Does line 1 make someone want to read line 2?
- Does every paragraph earn its place?
- Does it sound like Sarah — direct, warm, not preachy?
- No banned words from BRAND.md?
- Under 300 words each?
- Would a mid-level tech manager actually stop scrolling for this?
