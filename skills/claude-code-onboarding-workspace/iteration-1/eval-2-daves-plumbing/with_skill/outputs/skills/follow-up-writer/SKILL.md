# Follow-Up Sequence Writer

Write follow-up emails for Dave's Plumbing & Heating to chase quotes that haven't had a response.

This is Dave's most important skill — he loses half his jobs because he forgets to follow up. These emails need to be ready to copy, paste, and send with zero editing.

## Rules
- Always read BRAND.md for tone before writing
- Read BUSINESS.md for services context
- Write as Dave — first person, friendly, no-nonsense
- Never pushy — helpful and casual. Like a mate checking in, not a salesman chasing
- Each follow-up adds something new (don't just repeat "have you decided?")
- Keep emails short — 3-5 sentences max. Dave's customers are busy too
- British English only
- Always sign off with: Cheers, Dave
- Never use: "honestly", "cheap", "ASAP", "we regret to inform you"
- Always use natural phrases: "give me a shout", "happy to help", "no problem", "get it sorted"

## Sequence Pattern

### Email 1 — Day 2 (friendly check-in)
- Reference the specific job/quote
- Quick "just checking you got the quote alright"
- Offer to answer any questions
- Keep it to 3-4 sentences

### Email 2 — Day 7 (add value)
- Reference the job again
- Add something useful — mention availability, seasonal advice, or a practical tip
- Gentle nudge without pressure
- Keep it to 3-5 sentences

### Email 3 — Day 14 (final, keep the door open)
- Acknowledge they're probably busy
- No pressure — just letting them know the quote's still valid
- Leave the door open: "give me a shout whenever you're ready"
- Keep it to 2-3 sentences

## Output Format

For each email in the sequence:
1. **When to send** — e.g., "Day 2 after sending the quote"
2. **Subject line** — short, casual, no clickbait
3. **Body** — ready to copy and paste
4. **Sign-off** — always "Cheers, Dave"

## Example Context Dave Might Give
- "Chase the Johnson quote for the bathroom refit"
- "Haven't heard back from the boiler install on Elm Road"
- "Follow up that emergency call-out quote from last week"

Adapt the tone and detail to whatever Dave tells you about the job.
