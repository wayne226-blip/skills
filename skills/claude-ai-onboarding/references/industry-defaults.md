# Industry Defaults — claude.ai Project Onboarding

Use these defaults to pre-fill Project Instructions when client-specific data is incomplete. Client data always overrides these defaults.

### Estate Agents / Letting Agencies

**Terminology:**
vendor (not seller), instruct (not hire), applicant (not buyer in some contexts), property particulars, EPC, floorplan, chain-free, sole agency, under offer, SSTC, exchange, completion

**Compliance:**
Consumer Protection Regulations, Material Information requirements, Property Ombudsman, EPC disclosure, Money Laundering Regulations (ID checks)

**Default tone:**
Professional but warm, approachable. Confident without being pushy.

**Typical use cases:**
- Writing property descriptions from bullet points or notes
- Drafting viewing follow-up emails
- Vendor update emails (market feedback, price review)
- Social media posts (new listings, sold/let, market updates)
- Applicant matching emails
- Responding to online enquiries

**Banned phrases:**
"Don't miss out" (pressure selling), "investment opportunity" (unless regulated), "must see" (overused), "unique" (unless genuinely so)

**Key features:**
Two audiences — vendors (selling/letting) and applicants (buying/renting). Tone shifts slightly: more authoritative with vendors, more helpful/enthusiastic with applicants. Property descriptions need to be factual and comply with Material Information rules.

**Example prompts:**
1. "Write a property description for a 3-bed semi in E17 with a south-facing garden, new kitchen, and off-street parking"
2. "Draft a follow-up email to the Johnsons after their viewing at 14 Elm Road — they seemed interested but had concerns about the garden size"
3. "Write a vendor update for Mrs Chen — we've had 6 viewings in 2 weeks but no offers yet, market is slow"
4. "Create an Instagram post announcing we've just sold 22 Oak Lane — use a celebratory tone"
5. "Write a reply to this Rightmove enquiry asking about the ground rent on the flat in Forest Road"

### Accountants / Financial Services

**Terminology:**
Tax year, HMRC, self-assessment, corporation tax, VAT return, annual accounts, engagement letter, bookkeeping, payroll, dividend, director's loan, capital gains, allowable expenses, filing deadline, MTD (Making Tax Digital)

**Compliance:**
FCA if regulated, professional indemnity references, never guarantee financial outcomes, disclaimers on advice, anti-money laundering obligations, ICAEW/ACCA standards

**Default tone:**
Authoritative, precise, trustworthy. Clear without being condescending. Avoids jargon with clients unless they're financially literate.

**Typical use cases:**
- Client update emails (accounts ready, tax return filed)
- Tax deadline reminder emails
- Engagement letter writer
- Newsletter content (budget updates, tax tips)
- Report writer (management accounts commentary)
- Responding to client queries

**Banned phrases:**
"Guarantee" (re: financial outcomes), "promise" (re: savings/returns), "tax dodge" or "loophole" (use "tax-efficient" or "allowance")

**Key features:**
Seasonal awareness is critical — January (self-assessment deadline), April (new tax year), July (payments on account), September (MTD quarterly). Content should be timely. Clients range from sole traders to SME directors, so tone needs to flex.

**Example prompts:**
1. "Write an email to remind our sole trader clients that self-assessment is due by 31 January — friendly but firm"
2. "Draft a newsletter paragraph about the Spring Budget changes to capital gains tax"
3. "Write an engagement letter for a new limited company client — annual accounts and corporation tax return"
4. "Summarise this management accounts report into a plain English commentary for the client"
5. "Write a reply to a client asking whether they can claim their home office as an expense"

### Recruitment Agencies

**Terminology:**
Candidate, client, placement, perm, contract, day rate, notice period, CV, spec, briefing, shortlist, counteroffer, offer management, onboarding, temp, AWR, IR35

**Compliance:**
GDPR for candidate data, AWR for temps, IR35 awareness for contractors, REC Code of Practice

**Default tone:**
Energetic and consultative with clients (employers). Supportive and encouraging with candidates. Two distinct tones that switch depending on audience.

**Typical use cases:**
- Job advert writer
- Candidate outreach messages (LinkedIn/email)
- Client follow-up sequences
- LinkedIn posts (market insights, hiring trends)
- Interview confirmation emails
- Offer letters and onboarding emails

**Banned phrases:**
"Perfect candidate" (sets unrealistic expectations), age/gender/ethnicity references in job ads (discrimination), "young and dynamic team" (age discrimination)

**Key features:**
Dual tone is the most important feature — the skill needs to know whether it's writing to a client or a candidate and adjust accordingly. Job adverts must be inclusive and compliant. Speed matters in recruitment, so outputs should be concise and action-oriented.

**Example prompts:**
1. "Write a job advert for a Senior Accountant in Manchester, £55-65k, hybrid working, mid-size practice"
2. "Draft a LinkedIn message to a passive candidate about a Head of Marketing role — they're currently at a competitor"
3. "Write a follow-up email to the hiring manager at Acme Ltd — we sent 3 CVs last week and haven't heard back"
4. "Create a LinkedIn post about the current talent shortage in the tech sector — position us as experts"
5. "Write an interview confirmation email to Sarah for Thursday at 2pm at the client's office in Canary Wharf"

### Solicitors / Legal Practices

**Terminology:**
Matter, instruction, client care letter, completion, exchange, disbursements, conveyancing, litigation, tribunal, brief, counsel, disclosure, witness statement, consent order, decree

**Compliance:**
SRA Standards and Regulations, client money rules, complaints procedure (must reference in client care letters), conflict of interest checks, data protection, professional undertakings

**Default tone:**
Formal, measured, precise. Never vague. Empathetic where appropriate (family law, personal injury) but always professional. Legal precision is non-negotiable.

**Typical use cases:**
- Client care letters
- Matter update emails (progress on case)
- Internal file notes
- New enquiry responses
- Complaint handling letters
- Court/tribunal preparation summaries

**Banned phrases:**
"We guarantee" (re: case outcome), "you will win", "easy case", "no-brainer" — any language implying certainty of outcome. Avoid casual language in client correspondence.

**Key features:**
Legal precision is the defining requirement. Never use vague language in client communications. Different practice areas (conveyancing, family, commercial, litigation) have different terminology and tone nuances. Client care letters must include required SRA information.

**Example prompts:**
1. "Write a client care letter for a new residential conveyancing matter — purchase of 42 Maple Street for £375,000"
2. "Draft an update email to Mr and Mrs Patel — their house purchase has been delayed because the seller's solicitor hasn't responded to enquiries"
3. "Write an internal file note summarising today's call with the client about her divorce petition"
4. "Respond to a new enquiry about a commercial lease dispute — they found us on Google"
5. "Draft a complaint acknowledgement letter — the client is unhappy with how long their matter is taking"

### Retailers (Online / High Street)

**Terminology:**
SKU, product line, collection, seasonal, markdown, footfall (physical), conversion (online), basket value, upsell, cross-sell, drop, restock, launch, limited edition

**Compliance:**
Consumer Rights Act, distance selling regulations (online), returns policy references, advertising standards (ASA), pricing accuracy

**Default tone:**
Friendly, approachable, enthusiastic about products. Conversational but not sloppy. Matches the energy of the brand.

**Typical use cases:**
- Product description writer
- Customer reply handler (queries, complaints, reviews)
- Social media posts (Instagram/Facebook heavy)
- Newsletter/email campaign writer
- Review response handler (Google, Trustpilot, etc.)
- Seasonal campaign copy

**Banned phrases:**
"Cheap" (use "affordable" or "great value"), "buy now" in isolation without context (pushy), misleading discount claims

**Key features:**
Heavy visual/social focus — posts need to work alongside images. Product descriptions need to balance SEO with readability. Seasonal cycles drive content (Christmas, Valentine's, Summer, Black Friday). Online retailers need conversion-focused copy; high street needs footfall-driving copy.

**Example prompts:**
1. "Write a product description for our new hand-poured soy candle — lavender and vanilla, 40-hour burn time, £18.99"
2. "Reply to this 1-star Trustpilot review — the customer says their order arrived damaged"
3. "Write an Instagram caption for our new spring collection launch — we're a sustainable fashion brand"
4. "Draft a Black Friday email campaign — 20% off everything, free shipping over £50, runs Friday to Monday"
5. "Write a friendly reply to a customer asking if we can do gift wrapping"

### Tradespeople / Contractors

**Terminology:**
Quote, job, site, call-out, materials, labour, warranty, guarantee, first fix, second fix, snagging, sign-off, building regs, Part P, Gas Safe, NICEIC

**Compliance:**
Industry-specific certifications (Gas Safe, NICEIC, FGAS, etc.), building regulations, warranty terms, insurance references

**Default tone:**
Direct, no-nonsense, plain language. Friendly but professional. No waffle — these clients and their customers don't want essays.

**Typical use cases:**
- Quote follow-up emails ("just checking if you'd like to go ahead")
- Job confirmation messages
- Review response handler (Google, Checkatrade)
- Social media posts (before/after photos, completed jobs)
- Invoice chaser emails
- Warranty/guarantee letters

**Banned phrases:**
"Honestly" (implies you're sometimes dishonest), overpromising on timelines, technical jargon to customers (unless they're in the trade)

**Key features:**
Short, punchy communications. These clients don't have time for long emails. Before/after content works well on social media. Quotes and follow-ups need to be professional but not stuffy. Many tradespeople are sole traders — personal and business brand overlap.

**Example prompts:**
1. "Write a follow-up email to Mrs Thompson — I sent her a quote for a bathroom refit 5 days ago, £4,200, haven't heard back"
2. "Reply to this Google review — 5 stars, customer is really happy with their new kitchen"
3. "Write an Instagram post for a before/after of a full house rewire we just finished"
4. "Draft a polite invoice chaser — the Hendersons' invoice is 14 days overdue, £1,850 for a boiler install"
5. "Write a short warranty letter for a customer — 2-year guarantee on all workmanship for their loft conversion"

### Coaches / Consultants / Freelancers

**Terminology:**
Discovery call, programme, package, transformation, results, case study, testimonial, retainer, session, framework, methodology, niche, ideal client, funnel

**Compliance:**
Varies — some regulated (financial coaches, therapists), most not. Testimonial/results claims should include "results may vary" where appropriate. ICO registration if handling personal data.

**Default tone:**
Confident, personal, thought-leader positioning. Warm but authoritative. The person IS the brand.

**Typical use cases:**
- LinkedIn post writer (biggest channel for most coaches/consultants)
- Proposal/scope of work writer
- Follow-up sequences (post-discovery call, post-event)
- Blog/long-form content (thought leadership)
- Programme/package description writer
- Discovery call follow-up emails

**Banned phrases:**
"Pick my brain" (devalues expertise), "guru" or "ninja" (overused), income/results claims without evidence, "guaranteed transformation"

**Key features:**
Personal brand IS the business brand — ME.md and BRAND.md overlap significantly. LinkedIn is typically the primary platform. Content needs to establish authority while remaining approachable. The tone is more personal and story-driven than corporate.

**Example prompts:**
1. "Write a LinkedIn post about why most small businesses waste money on marketing they don't measure — tie it back to my analytics consulting service"
2. "Draft a proposal for a 3-month coaching programme — leadership development for a mid-size tech company, 6 sessions, £4,500"
3. "Write a follow-up email to Jane who had a discovery call yesterday — she's interested but needs to think about it"
4. "Create a blog post about the 3 biggest mistakes I see founders make with their pricing strategy"
5. "Write a programme description for my new group coaching offer — 8 weeks, weekly calls, WhatsApp support, limited to 10 people, £997"
